Find a price that works.

Every bull case for the AI build-out treats the agent's price as a free variable. It isn't. Firms only replace a worker when the agent is cheap enough to be worth the trouble — so adoption falls as price rises, and revenue is the product of two terms pulling against each other. That product has a maximum. Drag the price and watch it fall off on both sides. The question is whether the peak clears the capex.

What the model does

Revenue is the wage base times price times adoption, kept on frontier models by the centralization share κ. The move that matters is refusing to let price and adoption float independently — adoption is a function of price:

R(φ) = κ · N · w · φ · a(φ)   where  a(φ) = A · max(0, 1 − φ/φc)

Read the pieces left to right: κ is the fraction of spend that stays centralized rather than fleeing to open weights on a laptop; N is the number of workers; w is the average wage; φ is the agent's price as a share of that wage; and a(φ) is the fraction of the pool that adopts at that price. Adoption falls linearly from its ceiling A (the most that ever adopt, even at a price near zero) to zero at the walk-away price φc, where the agent stops being worth the disruption.

Where the ceiling comes from

The max(0, …) only exists to stop adoption going negative once the price passes φc. Inside the live range — prices from 0 to φc, the only part where anyone is buying — that term is already positive, so it drops away and revenue is a clean downward parabola in φ:

R(φ) = κ · A · N · w · φ · (1 − φ/φc)    for 0 ≤ φ ≤ φc  (and R = 0 beyond)

A downward parabola has a top. Bundle everything that isn't φ into a single constant C = κ·A·N·w, take the derivative, and set the slope to zero — that's the peak:

dR/dφ = C · (1 − 2φ/φc) = 0  ⟹  φ* = φc / 2

So the best price is always half the walk-away price: push toward free and you earn nothing per seat; push toward φc and nobody buys; the optimum sits dead center. Substitute φ* back in — at that price both φ and the adoption factor equal one-half — and you get a hard ceiling on what this pool can ever yield:

Rmax = C · (φc/2) · (1/2) = κ · A · N · w · φc / 4

The 4 is two halves multiplied: half from the optimal price being φc/2, half from adoption sitting at its midpoint when you're there. At the best possible price you capture exactly a quarter of κ·A·N·w·φc, and nothing does better.

The assumption that matters

That clean quarter is specific to the linear curve, and linear is the least generous of the three shapes — not the most. It assumes every firm shares one walk-away price, so adoption falls off a single cliff at φc. Real firms have a spread of walk-away prices, which fattens the shoulder of the curve and lets more of them keep buying at moderate prices. The Power and Logit shapes in the selector both capture that, and both lift the peak above the linear quarter — neither shape makes the argument easier for the bear.

The two are generous in different ways. Power holds adoption high across low-to-mid prices, so it tends to produce the tallest peak of the three. Logit is the standard economics demand curve and the most defensible: it never hits a hard zero, so a stubborn tail of high-tolerance firms always pays something. This is why the shape selector matters more than any single slider. If you want to stress-test the argument, run it under Power and Logit, not just linear — and see whether the peak clears the capex line. If even these generous curves can't get the peak far enough ahead of the spend, the price wall was never the weak point: the ceiling is structural.

That ceiling is the whole argument. The labs are not choosing between a good price and a bad one — they are choosing a point on a curve whose peak may already sit below the capex line, in which case no pricing strategy clears it. Not because the technology failed, but because the shape of the market forbids it. If you can find a combination of inputs — under any curve shape — that clears the line without every variable breaking favorably at once, you've found something I couldn't.
